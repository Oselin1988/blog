The 50 Million People Question: Are We Missing the Genetic Truth About Alzheimer's?

📅 20 July 2026 🏷️ Alzheimer's Genetics ⏱️ 8 min read 👩‍🔬 Linda Osaghale

Why Most Alzheimer's Genetic Studies Get It Wrong (And How We Fixed It)

Imagine being told you have a 50% chance of developing a disease that slowly steals your memories, your identity, and your independence. Now imagine that the scientific community can't agree on which genetic markers actually matter.

That's the frustrating reality of Alzheimer's disease research.

But here's the thing: it doesn't have to be this way.

The Problem Nobody's Talking About

Alzheimer's disease affects approximately 50 million people worldwide. That's more than the entire population of Spain, or roughly the number of people living in England and South Korea combined. By 2050, that number is expected to triple.

We've spent billions on research. We've sequenced millions of genomes. We've published thousands of studies.

Yet the genetic basis of Alzheimer's disease remains frustratingly unclear.

Why? Because most studies make a critical mistake: they rush to interpret findings before confirming they're actually real.

The "False Positive" Epidemic

Here's the uncomfortable truth that most researchers don't want to admit: many genetic associations don't replicate.

A study finds a "significant" association in one population. It makes headlines. It gets funding. But when another research group tries to confirm it in a different cohort, the association disappears.

This isn't just wasteful—it's dangerous. False leads divert resources away from genuine discoveries. They create confusion for patients and clinicians. And they delay the development of effective treatments.

What We Did Differently

In our recently published study, we took a radically different approach. Instead of rushing from discovery to interpretation, we built a replication-first framework.

Step 1: Discovery

We started with the FinnGen cohort—one of the largest population-based genomics projects in the world. Our discovery dataset included:

  • 211,678 individuals
  • 2,191 clinically-defined Alzheimer's cases
  • 209,487 controls
  • European ancestry (Finnish population)

Using standard genome-wide association study (GWAS) methods, we identified variants that reached genome-wide significance (P < 5 × 10⁻⁸).

What did we find?

Three variants. All on chromosome 19. All within the well-known APOE region.

The strongest signal came from rs429358, with an odds ratio of 4.58 (P = 5.20 × 10⁻¹⁹⁷). This means carriers have nearly five times the risk of developing Alzheimer's disease.

  • rs3178166 (OR = 0.80, P = 1.53 × 10⁻¹¹): protective effect
  • rs111371860 (OR = 1.44, P = 3.91 × 10⁻¹⁰): risk-increasing effect

Step 2: The "Wait, Let's Confirm This" Step

Here's where most studies would publish their findings and move on. But we didn't.

Instead, we took our three lead variants and tested them in two completely independent cohorts:

Replication Cohort 1 (IEU):

  • 488,285 individuals (954 cases, 487,331 controls)
  • European ancestry (UK Biobank)
  • Phenotype: Algorithm-defined Alzheimer's cases

Replication Cohort 2 (EBI):

  • 85,934 individuals (39,106 cases, 46,828 controls)
  • European ancestry
  • Phenotype: Clinically-diagnosed Alzheimer's disease

Step 3: The Moment of Truth

The results were compelling, though not perfectly uniform across all cohorts—which is exactly why replication matters.

In the IEU cohort, all three variants replicated successfully:

  • rs429358: P = 2.80 × 10⁻¹⁷⁵ (even stronger than discovery!)
  • rs111371860: P = 4.30 × 10⁻⁹
  • rs3178166: P = 3.90 × 10⁻³

In the EBI cohort, two of the three variants replicated strongly:

  • rs111371860: P = 1.37 × 10⁻⁴⁶ (astonishingly strong!)
  • rs3178166: P = 1.47 × 10⁻⁴

The third variant, rs429358, was not available in the EBI dataset due to quality control procedures—a common issue when working with the complex APOE region. This doesn't diminish the overall replication success; it simply highlights the technical challenges of genetic research.

What does this tell us?

First, the APOE locus is real. It's not a statistical fluke. It's not a cohort-specific artifact. This is a genuine, robust, and replicable genetic risk factor for Alzheimer's disease.

Second, replication works. By insisting on confirmation before interpretation, we've built genuine confidence in our findings.

And third, replication reveals nuance. The differences between cohorts—in effect sizes, phenotype definitions, and variant availability—show why we can't rely on a single study. We need multiple lines of evidence from diverse populations.

The Bottom Line

Alzheimer's disease is one of the most devastating disorders of our time. We need reliable answers, not false promises.

Our study shows that:

  • ✅ Replication matters - Confirming findings in independent cohorts builds genuine confidence
  • ✅ The APOE locus is robust - It replicates across multiple large, independent populations
  • ✅ There's more to discover - Gene-based analysis reveals loci masked by dominant signals

The 50 million people affected by Alzheimer's disease deserve nothing less than rigorous, reproducible science.
