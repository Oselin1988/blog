How Your Brain's Immune System Could Hold the Key to Stopping Alzheimer's

Your brain has its own immune system. These cells—called microglia—are the brain's first responders. They detect problems, clear debris, and protect against infection.

But what if these immune cells could be trained to fight Alzheimer's disease?

TREM2 might make that possible.

What We Still Don't Know

1. How Does TREM2 Work in Alzheimer's?

We know TREM2 is associated with Alzheimer's, but we don't fully understand how it works.

2. What's the Optimal TREM2 Level?

Too little TREM2 is harmful. Could too much TREM2 be harmful too?

3. How Does TREM2 Interact with APOE?

TREM2 and APOE may work together. The details are unclear.

4. Is TREM2 a Safe Therapeutic Target?

We need to understand the risks of TREM2-targeted therapies.

The Bottom Line

  • TREM2 is a true Alzheimer's risk gene (P = 2.10 × 10⁻⁸)
  • TREM2 is expressed on microglia—the brain's immune cells
  • TREM2 is essential for amyloid clearance
  • TREM2 modulates neuroinflammation
  • TREM2 agonists represent a promising therapeutic strategy

Understanding TREM2 could revolutionize Alzheimer's treatment through immune modulation.
